Our Story

How three people ended up raising $4.2 million to teach a computer about bread. A story of vision, ambition, and one badly sliced sourdough that changed everything.

The Origin

It started with a bad slice.
It will end with a revolution.*

*Definition of revolution subject to change. Current definition: "a software product that tells you how your bread is feeling."

In November 2022, our CEO Chad Grainsworth was making toast. He cut his sourdough at what he would later describe in a pitch deck as "a catastrophically suboptimal angle." The slice was uneven. One half was thick. The other was thin. It toasted unevenly. Chad ate it anyway because he was hungry, but he was thinking about it.

Chad had recently left his previous job at RipeAI, a startup that used computer vision to predict avocado ripeness. RipeAI had a 73% model accuracy, $12M in funding, and zero revenue. Chad was their most senior ML engineer. He left to "pursue something more meaningful." He ate the toast. He stared at the ceiling. He thought: "What if the problem with bread isn't bread? What if the problem is that bread doesn't know anything?"

He texted his friend Preethi Doughrani at 11:43 PM: "I think bread needs AI." She replied: "chad it's midnight." He replied: "I know but think about it." She thought about it for four minutes and then replied: "okay I'm in." This is how great companies are founded.

They brought in Dmitri Kvasov the following Tuesday. Dmitri had previously co-founded two startups. Both had pivoted to unrelated industries before shutting down. He was described by one former investor as "someone who has never met a problem he didn't want to overcomplicate." He baked sourdough as a hobby. He had a Reddit account exclusively for posting bread photos. His starter, Leviticus, was two years old and doing better than any of his companies. He joined without negotiating. There was nothing to negotiate — there is no salary.

Six months later, they had: a company name, a Delaware LLC, a Notion workspace with 47 pages of product spec, 14 billion training images, a SmartKnife prototype that kept disconnecting from Wi-Fi, and $4.2 million in seed funding from investors who described the pitch as "either brilliant or very confident." They have not determined which. The investors have not either. Everyone is proceeding on the assumption that it doesn't matter.

"Bread has been around for 14,000 years. Nobody has asked it how it's doing. We are going to ask it how it's doing. That's the company." — Chad Grainsworth, CEO. This is the actual mission statement.
Values

Six values. Two of them are about bread. All of them are about bread.

🌾

Grain-First Thinking

Every decision starts with: "Is this good for bread?" If the answer is unclear, we bake a loaf and think about it until the answer becomes clear. This process takes between 45 minutes and three weeks, depending on the complexity of the decision and whether Leviticus is feeling active.

😭

Radical Bread Empathy

We believe bread has an inner life that has been ignored for 14,000 years. We are correcting that. Our CSA (Crust Sentiment Analysis) system was built on the premise that bread deserves to be understood. Chad got emotional during the demo at our seed pitch. Not performatively. He just really feels strongly about this.

📐

Precision or Nothing

We measure slice angles to ±0.003mm. Our board asked if this level of precision is "commercially relevant." We said the market would decide. The market has not yet decided because we have no customers. But we remain committed to the ±0.003mm.

🚀

Move Fast, Proof Slow

As a company, we move fast. Features ship weekly. Decks are made overnight. Emails are sent immediately. But we believe great sourdough requires a 72-hour cold ferment and you cannot rush it. We hold both of these truths simultaneously and it creates a company culture that is very hard to describe to new hires.

💀

Default to Optimism

We are pre-revenue, our lead investor has stopped responding to Slack messages, our staleness model only predicts Thursdays, and we once accidentally baked three sensors into a test loaf. We are still very optimistic. We think this says a lot about our culture. Others have described it as "a coping mechanism."

🍞

Bread Is Enough

We have been asked many times why we don't expand to other baked goods. Croissants. Bagels. Cakes. Our answer: we tried. We ran a croissant through BreadGPT. The model diagnosed it as "deeply misunderstood." The crust sentiment was "dissociating." We are staying in our lane. The lane is bread.
